## Music by Percy Bysshe Shelley

```
I pant for the music which is divine,
My heart in its thirst is a dying flower;
Pour forth the sound like enchanted wine,
Loosen the notes in a silver shower;
Like a herbless plain, for the gentle rain,
I gasp, I faint, till they wake again.

Let me drink of the spirit of that sweet sound,
More, oh more,--I am thirsting yet;
It loosens the serpent which care has bound
Upon my heart to stifle it;
The dissolving strain, through every vein,
Passes into my heart and brain.

As the scent of a violet withered up,
Which grew by the brink of a silver lake,
When the hot noon has drained its dewy cup,
And mist there was none its thirst to slake--
And the violet lay dead while the odour flew
On the wings of the wind o'er the waters blue--

As one who drinks from a charmed cup
Of foaming, and sparkling, and murmuring wine,
Whom, a mighty Enchantress filling up,
Invites to love with her kiss divine...
```

### Form, Structure, Meter, and Rhyme

In this poem, Percy Bysshe Shelley utilizes a lyrical and flowing structure with a consistent ABAB rhyme scheme. The poem consists of four stanzas, each with six lines. The meter is primarily iambic tetrameter, with lines alternating between eight and six syllables. This rhythmic pattern, along with the rhyme scheme, contributes to the musical quality of the poem, mirroring the theme of music and its intoxicating effect.

### Themes and Imagery

The central theme of the poem is the transformative power of music on the soul. Through vivid imagery, Shelley portrays music as a life-giving force that rejuvenates and liberates the speaker from the burdens of everyday life. The imagery of a dying flower longing for rain, a withered violet, and a charmed cup of wine all symbolize the speaker's thirst for the transcendent experience that music provides. The enchanting and intoxicating nature of music is depicted through sensory details that evoke a sense of longing and ecstasy.

### Literary Devices

Shelley employs a variety of literary devices to enhance the emotional impact of the poem. The use of similes, such as "Like a herbless plain, for the gentle rain," and personification, such as "Loosen the notes in a silver shower," create vivid and evocative imagery that captures the transformative power of music. Additionally, the repetition of the word "more" in the second stanza emphasizes the speaker's insatiable thirst for the music's divine essence. The metaphor of the serpent being loosened by the music symbolizes the release of inner turmoil and emotional repression.

### Historical and Personal Context

Percy Bysshe Shelley was a Romantic poet who believed in the power of art and creativity to evoke profound emotions and inspire social change. This poem reflects Shelley's own love for music and the arts, as well as his belief in the transcendent power of beauty to uplift the human spirit. Shelley's personal struggles with love, loss, and existential questions are echoed in the speaker's yearning for the transformative experience that music provides.

### Deep Reading

At its core, "Music" by Percy Bysshe Shelley is a meditation on the spiritual and emotional impact of art. The speaker's intense longing for the divine essence of music mirrors humanity's universal desire for transcendence and connection to something greater than oneself. Through the imagery of a dying flower, a withered violet, and a charmed cup of wine, Shelley conveys the transformative power of music to awaken the soul and elevate the spirit. The poem serves as a testament to the enduring power of art to inspire, uplift, and transform the human experience.

## Sonnet 09 - Can it be right to give what I can give? by Elizabeth Barrett Browning


## Love and Friendship by Emily Bronte

```
Love is like the wild rose-briar,
Friendship like the holly-tree --
The holly is dark when the rose-briar blooms
But which will bloom most contantly?
The wild-rose briar is sweet in the spring,
Its summer blossoms scent the air;
Yet wait till winter comes again
And who wil call the wild-briar fair?
Then scorn the silly rose-wreath now
And deck thee with the holly's sheen,
That when December blights thy brow
He may still leave thy garland green.
```

### Form, Structure, Meter, and Rhyme

In terms of form, Emily Bronte's poem "Love and Friendship" consists of a quatrain with a rhyme scheme of ABAB. The poem is structured around the comparison between love and friendship, with each stanza presenting a contrast between the two. The meter is primarily iambic tetrameter, with lines alternating between four and three stressed syllables. This creates a rhythmic and flowing quality to the poem, enhancing its lyrical nature.

### Themes and Imagery

The central theme of the poem revolves around the contrasting nature of love and friendship. Bronte uses the imagery of the wild rose-briar and the holly-tree to symbolize these two concepts. The wild rose-briar represents love, with its fleeting beauty and sweetness in spring, while the holly-tree symbolizes friendship, which remains constant and steadfast even in the harshest of conditions. The imagery of winter and December conveys the idea of enduring through difficult times, with the holly's green garland symbolizing the resilience of true friendship.

### Literary Devices

Bronte employs several literary devices in the poem to enhance its meaning and impact. One such device is metaphor, as seen in the comparison between love and the wild rose-briar, and friendship and the holly-tree. The use of personification is evident in lines such as "And deck thee with the holly's sheen," giving human-like qualities to the plants. Additionally, the poem uses contrast and imagery to highlight the differences between love's transient nature and friendship's enduring strength.

### Historical and Personal Context

Emily Bronte, known for her introspective and emotionally charged poetry, likely drew inspiration from her own experiences and observations of relationships for this poem. The themes of love and friendship were common in Romantic literature of the time, reflecting the era's focus on emotions and personal connections. Bronte's use of nature imagery also aligns with the Romantic tradition of finding beauty and meaning in the natural world.

### Deep Reading

On a deeper level, "Love and Friendship" can be interpreted as a reflection on the complexities of human relationships. Bronte suggests that while love may be fleeting and superficial, true friendship endures through all seasons of life. The poem encourages readers to value the steadfastness and reliability of friendship over the transient allure of romantic love. By using the symbolism of plants and seasons, Bronte conveys a timeless message about the importance of loyalty and support in relationships.
